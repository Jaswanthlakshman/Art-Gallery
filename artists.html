Vincent van Gogh

Post-Impressionist Painter

1853 - 1890

  • Biography
  • Arts

Biography

Vincent Willem van Gogh was a Dutch Post-Impressionist painter who is among the most famous and influential figures in the history of Western art. In just over a decade he created approximately 2100 artworks, including around 860 oil paintings, most of them in the last two years of his life.

Van Gogh was born into an upper-middle-class family. As a young man he worked as an art dealer, often traveling, but became depressed after he was transferred to London. He turned to religion and spent time as a Protestant missionary in southern Belgium.

His depression persisted, and on 27 July 1890, Van Gogh is believed to have shot himself in the chest with a revolver, dying from his injuries two days later.

Biography

Oscar-Claude Monet was a French painter and founder of impressionist painting who is seen as a key precursor to modernism, especially in his attempts to paint nature as he perceived it. During his long career, he was the most consistent and prolific practitioner of impressionism's philosophy of expressing one's perceptions before nature, especially as applied to plein air (outdoor) landscape painting.

Monet's ambition of documenting the French countryside led him to adopt a method of painting the same scene many times in order to capture the changing of light and the passing of the seasons. From 1883, Monet lived in Giverny, where he purchased a house and property and began a vast landscaping project which included lily ponds that would become the subjects of his best-known works.

Biography

Pablo Ruiz Picasso was a Spanish painter, sculptor, printmaker, ceramicist and theatre designer who spent most of his adult life in France. Regarded as one of the most influential artists of the 20th century, he is known for co-founding the Cubist movement, the invention of constructed sculpture, the co-invention of collage, and for the wide variety of styles that he helped develop and explore.

Picasso demonstrated extraordinary artistic talent in his early years, painting in a naturalistic manner through his childhood and adolescence. During the first decade of the 20th century, his style changed as he experimented with different theories, techniques, and ideas. After 1906, the Fauvist work of the slightly older artist Henri Matisse motivated Picasso to explore more radical styles, beginning a fruitful rivalry between the two artists, who subsequently were often paired by critics as the leaders of modern art.

Frida Kahlo

Biography

Magdalena Carmen Frida Kahlo y Calderón was a Mexican painter known for her many portraits, self-portraits, and works inspired by the nature and artifacts of Mexico. Inspired by the country's popular culture, she employed a naïve folk art style to explore questions of identity, postcolonialism, gender, class, and race in Mexican society.

Kahlo's paintings often had strong autobiographical elements and mixed realism with fantasy. In addition to belonging to the post-revolutionary Mexicayotl movement, which sought to define a Mexican identity, Kahlo has been described as a surrealist or magical realist. She is known for painting about her experience of chronic pain.

Kahlo had a volatile marriage with the famous Mexican artist Diego Rivera. She suffered lifelong health problems, many of which stemmed from a traffic accident she experienced as a teenager. These issues are reflected in her works, more than half of which are self-portraits of one sort or another.

The Starry Night

by Vincent van Gogh

$100,000,000

Year: 1889

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 73.7 cm × 92.1 cm (29 in × 36 1/4 in)

Location: Museum of Modern Art, New York

Style: Post-Impressionism

The Starry Night is an oil-on-canvas painting by the Dutch Post-Impressionist painter Vincent van Gogh. Painted in June 1889, it depicts the view from the east-facing window of his asylum room at Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, just before sunrise, with the addition of an imaginary village.

The painting has been in the permanent collection of the Museum of Modern Art in New York City since 1941, acquired through the Lillie P. Bliss Bequest. It is regarded as among Van Gogh's finest works and is one of the most recognized paintings in the history of Western culture.

The painting's striking blues and yellows and the dreamy, swirling atmosphere have intrigued art lovers for decades. Van Gogh's night sky is a field of roiling energy. Below the exploding stars, the village is a place of quiet order.

Sunflowers

by Vincent van Gogh

$85,000,000

Year: 1888

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 92.1 cm × 73 cm (36.2 in × 28.7 in)

Location: National Gallery, London

Style: Post-Impressionism

Sunflowers is the title of two series of still life paintings by Vincent van Gogh. The first series, executed in Paris in 1887, depicts the flowers lying on the ground. The second set, created a year later in Arles, shows a bouquet of sunflowers in a vase.

Van Gogh's sunflowers were painted to decorate the Yellow House in Arles in preparation for his friend Paul Gauguin's visit. The paintings show sunflowers in all stages of life, from full bloom to withering. The vibrant yellow palette reflects Van Gogh's fascination with color symbolism.

These paintings are among Van Gogh's most famous and are frequently reproduced. They demonstrate his innovative use of thick brushstrokes (impasto) and his bold, expressive color choices.

Self-Portrait

by Vincent van Gogh

$75,000,000

Year: 1889

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 65 cm × 54 cm (25.6 in × 21.3 in)

Location: Musée d'Orsay, Paris

Style: Post-Impressionism

Vincent van Gogh created more than 30 self-portraits between 1886 and 1889. His collection of self-portraits places him among the most prolific self-portraitists of all time. This particular self-portrait was painted in September 1889, shortly after he left the hospital in Arles.

The painting is notable for its striking color contrasts between the blue-green background and the orange-red tones of Van Gogh's hair and beard. His piercing gaze and the intensity of expression reveal his psychological state during this turbulent period of his life.

Van Gogh's self-portraits were often created as studies of his own psychological condition and as exercises in technique. He couldn't afford models, so he painted himself as a form of practice and self-examination.

The Potato Eaters

by Vincent van Gogh

$65,000,000

Year: 1885

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 82 cm × 114 cm (32 in × 45 in)

Location: Van Gogh Museum, Amsterdam

Style: Realism

The Potato Eaters is an oil painting by Vincent van Gogh painted in April 1885 in Nuenen, Netherlands. It is considered one of Van Gogh's first major works and his earliest masterpiece. The painting depicts five peasants sitting around a square table eating potatoes.

Van Gogh wanted to portray the peasants as they really were, deliberately choosing coarse and ugly models who he thought would be natural and unspoiled in his finished work. The painting is dark and somber, reflecting the difficult conditions of peasant life.

This work represents Van Gogh's attempt to establish himself as a painter of rural life and peasant character. It shows his early interest in depicting working-class subjects, which would continue throughout his career.

Impression, Sunrise

by Claude Monet

$60,000,000

Year: 1872

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 48 cm × 63 cm (19 in × 25 in)

Location: Musée Marmottan Monet, Paris

Style: Impressionism

Impression, Sunrise is the painting that gave the Impressionist movement its name. Depicting the port of Le Havre at sunrise, the work is characterized by loose brushwork and atmospheric effects rather than precise details.

When exhibited in 1874, the painting was criticized for its unfinished appearance. Art critic Louis Leroy derisively used the term "Impressionism" from the painting's title, which the artists later adopted as their movement's name.

The painting demonstrates Monet's revolutionary approach to capturing light and atmosphere. The orange sun reflected in the water creates a vibrant focal point amidst the hazy blue-gray tones of the harbor scene.

Water Lilies

by Claude Monet

$54,000,000

Year: 1919

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 100 cm × 200 cm (39 in × 79 in)

Location: Musée de l'Orangerie, Paris

Style: Impressionism

Water Lilies is a series of approximately 250 oil paintings by Claude Monet, depicting his flower garden at Giverny. Painted during the last 30 years of his life, these works focus on the water lily pond under varying light and weather conditions.

Monet's later works, including the Water Lilies series, are characterized by loose brushwork and a dreamy, almost abstract quality. The paintings often lack a horizon line, immersing the viewer in the reflective surface of the water.

These works represent Monet's lifelong fascination with light and its changing qualities, as well as his mastery of color harmonies. The large-scale panels in the Musée de l'Orangerie were specially designed to create an immersive environment.

Woman with a Parasol

by Claude Monet

$45,000,000

Year: 1875

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 100 cm × 81 cm (39 in × 32 in)

Location: National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C.

Style: Impressionism

Woman with a Parasol - Madame Monet and Her Son depicts Monet's wife Camille and their son Jean during a windy summer day in Argenteuil. The painting captures a fleeting moment with spontaneity and vibrant brushwork.

This work exemplifies Monet's ability to convey movement, light, and atmosphere. The parasol casts subtle shadows on Camille's face, while her billowing dress and the swirling clouds suggest a breeze. The low viewpoint gives the figure a monumental presence against the sky.

The painting demonstrates Monet's signature Impressionist style, with visible brushstrokes and attention to the effects of natural light. It remains one of his most beloved and recognizable works.

Guernica

by Pablo Picasso

$200,000,000

Year: 1937

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 349 cm × 776 cm (137.4 in × 305.5 in)

Location: Museo Reina Sofía, Madrid

Style: Cubism

Guernica is a large 1937 oil painting on canvas by Spanish artist Pablo Picasso. It is one of his best-known works, regarded by many art critics as the most moving and powerful anti-war painting in history. It is exhibited in the Museo Reina Sofía in Madrid.

The painting was created in response to the bombing of Guernica, a Basque Country town in northern Spain, by Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy at the request of the Spanish Nationalists. Upon completion, Guernica was exhibited at the Spanish display at the 1937 Paris International Exposition, and then at other venues around the world.

The painting's monochromatic palette, intense contrast, and large scale contribute to its dramatic impact. The complex composition, with its tortured figures and animals, presents a powerful condemnation of war and violence. Picasso's use of Cubist techniques to depict the human and animal forms creates a sense of fragmentation that mirrors the destruction of war.

Les Demoiselles d'Avignon

by Pablo Picasso

$150,000,000

Year: 1907

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 243.9 cm × 233.7 cm (96 in × 92 in)

Location: Museum of Modern Art, New York

Style: Proto-Cubism

This revolutionary work depicts five nude female prostitutes in a brothel, with figures rendered in jagged, disjointed shapes. Two figures exhibit African mask-like features, reflecting Picasso's fascination with tribal art.

The painting broke nearly every convention of Western art at the time - abandoning perspective, distorting figures, and incorporating primitive art influences. It marked the beginning of Cubism and radically changed the course of modern art.

Originally considered shocking and controversial, it's now recognized as one of the most important works in the development of modern art. The angular, fragmented composition creates a sense of psychological tension and raw energy.

The Weeping Woman

by Pablo Picasso

$80,000,000

Year: 1937

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 60 cm × 49 cm (23.6 in × 19.3 in)

Location: Tate Modern, London

Style: Cubism/Surrealism

This portrait of Dora Maar, Picasso's lover and muse, represents the suffering of women during the Spanish Civil War. The distorted face, with its jagged lines and contrasting colors, conveys intense emotional pain.

Part of Picasso's response to the bombing of Guernica, the work transforms human suffering into a cubist composition where the woman's features are fragmented yet express profound grief. The handkerchief clutched to her face appears to merge with her skin.

The vibrant colors - acid greens, purples, and yellows - create visual tension, while the sharp angles of the composition heighten the sense of distress. This is considered one of Picasso's most powerful depictions of human suffering.

Henry Ford Hospital

by Frida Kahlo

$25,000,000

Year: 1932

Medium: Oil on metal

Dimensions: 38.1 cm × 30.5 cm (15 in × 12 in)

Location: Dolores Olmedo Museum, Mexico City

Style: Surrealism/Naïve art

This deeply personal work depicts Kahlo's experience of miscarriage at Henry Ford Hospital in Detroit. The painting shows Kahlo bleeding on a hospital bed, connected by red ribbons to six floating images representing her trauma.

The symbolic elements include a fetus (her lost child), a pelvic bone (referencing her childhood accident), a medical model of female anatomy, an orchid (a gift from Diego Rivera), and a machine (representing Detroit's industrialization).

Painted in Kahlo's signature style blending realism with symbolic elements, the work is a powerful expression of physical and emotional pain. The barren industrial landscape in the background contrasts with the intensely personal foreground imagery.

Self-Portrait with Thorn Necklace and Hummingbird

by Frida Kahlo

$30,000,000

Year: 1940

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 61 cm × 47 cm (24 in × 18.5 in)

Location: Harry Ransom Center, Austin, Texas

Style: Surrealism/Naïve art

This iconic self-portrait shows Kahlo wearing a thorn necklace that draws blood, with a dead hummingbird hanging from it. Butterflies and a black cat flank her head, while a monkey plays with the thorn necklace.

The thorns represent the pain of her recent divorce from Diego Rivera, while the hummingbird (normally a symbol of luck in Mexican folklore) appears lifeless, suggesting her emotional state. The butterflies symbolize resurrection.

Kahlo's steady gaze challenges the viewer despite her obvious pain. The lush green background and white leaves frame her face, drawing attention to her stoic expression amidst suffering. This work exemplifies Kahlo's unique blend of personal symbolism and Mexican cultural references.

The Two Fridas

by Frida Kahlo

$35,000,000

Year: 1939

Medium: Oil on canvas

Dimensions: 173.5 cm × 173 cm (68.3 in × 68 in)

Location: Museo de Arte Moderno, Mexico City

Style: Surrealism

The Two Fridas is a double self-portrait by Mexican artist Frida Kahlo. The painting was created in 1939, the same year Kahlo divorced Diego Rivera, and is believed to be an expression of her feelings at the time.

The painting depicts two versions of Kahlo sitting side by side, with their hands linked. One Frida wears a white European-style dress (representing the Frida that Diego rejected), while the other wears a traditional Tehuana dress (representing the Frida that Diego loved). Both Fridas have exposed hearts, and the heart of the traditional Frida is healthy and whole, while the heart of the European Frida is broken and bleeding.

The painting is rich in symbolism, with the stormy sky in the background reflecting Kahlo's inner turmoil, and the surgical pincers held by the traditional Frida suggesting her attempts to stem the flow of blood from her wounded heart. The painting is considered one of Kahlo's most iconic works and a masterpiece of 20th-century art.
